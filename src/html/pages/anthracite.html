anthracite

Anthracite first allowed replacing natural gas and also to use non-coking coals instead of coke.

Thus, the productivity of the blast furnace is improved and the cost of the final product as well as the environmental impact are reduced. Therefore, anthracite is a cost-efficient substitute for coke in processes such as sintering, pelletizing and pulverized coal injection.

Anthracite size 13-25mm is also used as a reductant, mainly replacing coke for the production of alloys, calcium carbide. Bulk ferroalloys are alloys of iron and various metals largely consumed by the steel industry as well as in production of superalloys, aluminium, copper, chemicals and electronics. In steel, alloys are used to add the elements for manipulating strength and corrosion resistance in the final products. Manganese (45%), chromium (27%) and silicon (28%) are the key bulk alloys. Most ferroalloys (approximately 95%) are produced by smelting in an electric arc furnace with a carbon reductant. Production of ferroalloys is centered in China, with major contributions from CIS, India, Norway and South Africa.

Calcium carbide is produced in electric arc furnaces and is largely consumed by the steel industry to remove sulphur during steelmaking. Anthracite size 25-80mm is also used as a reductant, mainly replacing coke for the sugar and soda plant. A carbon source is used to generate carbon dioxide for the process of refining sugar. Smelting of ilmenite (an important titanium mineral) also consumes anthracite, using electric arc furnaces. This is a small market, with only a small number of smelters, notably in South Africa, Canada and Russia. This is still a market for anthracite because the process requires high quality reductant which anthracite can provide.
